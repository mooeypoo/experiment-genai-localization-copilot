## 2) Decisions & Rationale

**Locale Code: zh-CN**
Chose 'zh-CN' as the locale identifier following BCP 47 standard:
- 'zh' = Chinese language code (ISO 639-1)
- 'CN' = Mainland China region code (ISO 3166-1)
- Distinguishes from Traditional Chinese (zh-TW for Taiwan, zh-HK for Hong Kong)
- Standard convention used by browsers, i18n libraries, and major platforms

Alternative considered: 'zh' alone (rejected—ambiguous between Simplified and Traditional variants).

**Simplified vs Traditional Chinese**
Chose Simplified Chinese for initial Chinese support:
- Most widely used variant globally (~1.3 billion users in mainland China, Singapore, Malaysia)
- Standard for most international applications serving Chinese markets
- Digital content in China predominantly uses Simplified Chinese
- Simpler character sets, easier to input and render

Traditional Chinese (繁體中文) support could be added later for Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau markets if needed.

**Translation Approach: Natural Conversational Tone**
Translated with natural, modern Simplified Chinese rather than literal word-for-word:
- "信号" (xìnhào) for "Signal" - means signal/sign, fits the brand
- "简介" (jiǎnjiè) for "Bio" - concise introduction
- "查看身份" (chákàn shēnfèn) for "Viewing as" - conveys role-based viewing
- "TA" for gender-neutral they/them pronouns (modern inclusive usage)

Avoided overly formal or bureaucratic Chinese; kept tone friendly and accessible.

**Brand Name Preservation**
Kept "Pulseboard" in English rather than translating or transliterating:
- Brand names typically stay in original language in Chinese contexts
- Chinese users familiar with English brand names in tech products
- Transliteration (脉搏板 màibō bǎn) would be awkward and unfamiliar
- Mixing English brand names with Chinese UI is standard practice

Other preserved elements: User names, locations, handles remain in English (user-generated content).

**Pronoun Translation Strategy**
Chinese pronouns differ fundamentally from English:
- 她 (tā, "she") - feminine pronoun, sounds identical to 他 but different character
- 他 (tā, "he") - masculine/general pronoun, traditionally gender-neutral
- TA - modern gender-neutral pronoun emerging in progressive Chinese communities
  - Written in Roman letters, not Chinese characters
  - Represents 他/她/它 (he/she/it) inclusively
  - Used in LGBTQ+ communities and inclusive spaces in China
- 任何代词 (rènhé dàicí) for "any pronouns" - literally "any pronouns"

Rationale for TA:
- Traditional 他们 (tāmen, "they") is plural, not singular non-binary
- TA is the most recognized singular gender-neutral form in modern Chinese
- Represents progressive, inclusive values fitting the app's ethos
- Used by younger generations and in social media contexts

Alternative considered: 他/她 (tā/tā) side-by-side (rejected—awkward, not truly neutral).

**Pluralization Handling**
Chinese doesn't inflect nouns for plural the same way English does:
- No -s ending for plurals
- Count + measure word + noun pattern (e.g., 三条评论 "3 [classifier] comments")
- Same text used for singular and plural in translation files

Vue i18n pluralization for Chinese:
```json
"commentsCount": "{count} 条评论 | {count} 条评论 | {count} 条评论"
```
All three plural forms identical because Chinese doesn't distinguish grammatically.

The 条 (tiáo) is a measure word used for long, narrow things (including comments, messages).

**Character Counter Terminology**
Used natural Chinese phrasing for character limits:
- "（超出 {count} 个字符）" - "(exceeded {count} characters)"
- "（剩余 {count} 个字符）" - "(remaining {count} characters)"
- 个 (gè) is the general measure word for counting characters

Chinese characters are typically counted the same way as English characters in digital contexts, even though one Chinese character conveys more meaning than one English letter.

**Typography Considerations**
No CSS changes needed, but key differences to note:
- Chinese characters are generally square/wider than Latin characters
- Line height: Chinese works well with 1.5 line-height from existing CSS
- Font fallback: System fonts (PingFang SC, Microsoft YaHei, etc.) handle Chinese automatically
- No explicit Chinese font specification needed—browser defaults work well

**Word Breaking and Wrapping**
Chinese text has unique line-breaking behavior:
- No spaces between words (unlike English)
- Can break between any two characters
- CSS `word-break: normal` handles this automatically
- Our existing `word-wrap: break-word` works correctly for Chinese

No CSS adjustments needed—modern browsers handle Chinese text wrapping correctly by default.

**Kicker Translation**
Translated "Signal" as "信号" (xìnhào):
- Direct translation meaning "signal" or "sign"
- Commonly used in tech/communication contexts
- Fits the brand's theme of small signals and observation
- Natural and recognizable to Chinese speakers

**Navigation and UI Elements**
Translated navigation tersely to match Chinese conventions:
- "帖子" (tiēzi) for "Post" - common term for social media posts
- "用户" (yònghù) for "Users" - standard term for users
- Chinese UI typically more concise than English equivalents
- Shorter translations help with responsive design

**Form Field Labels**
Kept labels concise following Chinese UI conventions:
- "显示名称" (xiǎnshì míngchēng) for "Display name"
- "人称代词" (rénchēng dàicí) for "Pronouns" - grammatically correct term
- "简介（可选）" (jiǎnjiè [kěxuǎn]) for "Bio (optional)" - parenthetical retained

Chinese users expect brief, direct labels in forms.

**Measure Words (Classifiers)**
Used appropriate measure words throughout:
- 条 (tiáo) for comments/messages (for long, narrow things)
- 人 (rén) for people (no separate measure word needed)
- 个 (gè) for characters (general classifier)

Measure words are integral to Chinese grammar for counting.

**Cultural Context for "Community"**
Translated "Community" as "社区" (shèqū):
- Standard term for community/neighborhood
- Used in social media contexts (e.g., WeChat communities)
- Warm, inclusive connotation
- Familiar to Chinese users in digital contexts

Alternative considered: 群体 (qúntǐ, "group") (rejected—too formal, less friendly).

## 3) Ambiguities Encountered

**Traditional vs Simplified Chinese?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Support only Simplified Chinese (zh-CN)
  - (b) Support only Traditional Chinese (zh-TW)
  - (c) Support both with separate translation files
- Resolution: Simplified Chinese only (option a)
- Rationale: Larger user base, standard for international apps, simpler starting point. Traditional Chinese can be added later if there's demand from Taiwan/Hong Kong users.

**Which gender-neutral pronoun to use?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) TA (modern inclusive pronoun in Roman letters)
  - (b) 他/她 (he/she side by side)
  - (c) 他们 (tāmen, "they" plural)
  - (d) X也 or other neo-pronouns
- Resolution: TA (option a)
- Challenge: Gender-neutral pronouns are nascent in Chinese; no universal standard
- Rationale: TA is the most recognized progressive form, used in LGBTQ+ communities, represents inclusivity, familiar to younger Chinese speakers.

**Should brand names be transliterated?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Keep "Pulseboard" in English
  - (b) Transliterate: 脉搏板 (màibō bǎn)
  - (c) Create a Chinese brand name
- Resolution: Keep English (option a)
- Rationale: Tech brands typically remain in English in Chinese contexts; transliteration sounds awkward; users expect English brand names.

**How to handle character counting?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Count Chinese characters same as English (1 character = 1 unit)
  - (b) Different character limits for Chinese
  - (c) Count by UTF-8 bytes
- Resolution: Same counting (option a)
- Rationale: One Chinese character = one character unit in the counter; simpler, consistent across languages; Chinese is more information-dense so same limit is actually generous.

**Language name in switcher?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) "简体中文" (Simplified Chinese in Chinese)
  - (b) "中文" (Chinese, ambiguous)
  - (c) "Chinese (Simplified)" in English
  - (d) "中文 (简体)" (Chinese with simplified note)
- Resolution: "简体中文" (option a)
- Rationale: Follows pattern of showing language names in their native script; clear and unambiguous; matches other language entries.

**Locale code format?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) 'zh-CN' (BCP 47 standard)
  - (b) 'zh_CN' (POSIX style)
  - (c) 'zh' (language only)
- Resolution: 'zh-CN' (option a)
- Rationale: BCP 47 is standard for web i18n; Vue i18n expects BCP 47 format; consistent with industry practice; allows future zh-TW, zh-HK variants.

**Should we add Chinese-specific fonts?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Add explicit Chinese font stack (Noto Sans SC, PingFang SC, etc.)
  - (b) Rely on system font fallback
  - (c) Load Google Fonts with Chinese support
- Resolution: Rely on system fallback (option b)
- Rationale: Modern browsers handle Chinese fonts well automatically; explicit fonts increase page weight significantly (Chinese fonts are large); system fonts optimized for each OS.

**How much conciseness in Chinese translations?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Translate word-for-word maintaining English length
  - (b) Use terser Chinese following native UI conventions
  - (c) Add explanatory text for clarity
- Resolution: Terse following conventions (option b)
- Rationale: Chinese UI is typically more concise than English; users expect brief labels; over-explanation feels awkward in Chinese.

**Should Chinese use different pluralization rules?**
- Options considered:
  - (a) Same text for all plural forms (current)
  - (b) Different phrasing for 0, 1, 2+ cases
  - (c) No pluralization at all
- Resolution: Same text for all (option a)
- Rationale: Chinese doesn't grammatically distinguish singular/plural; using same translation for all cases is correct; measure words handle quantity distinctions.

## 4) Tradeoffs

**Simplified Only vs Supporting Both Variants:**
- Chose Simplified Chinese only
- Tradeoff: Excludes Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau users who use Traditional
- Benefit: Simpler maintenance, covers majority of Chinese speakers, cleaner implementation

**TA Pronoun vs Traditional Approach:**
- Used modern TA for gender-neutral pronouns
- Tradeoff: Not universally recognized, might confuse older Chinese users
- Benefit: Progressive, inclusive, represents modern values, familiar to target demographic (younger tech users)

**English Brand Names vs Transliteration:**
- Kept "Pulseboard" in English
- Tradeoff: Might look inconsistent in all-Chinese UI
- Benefit: Authentic, matches real-world usage, avoids awkward transliterations

**System Fonts vs Custom Fonts:**
- Relied on system font fallback for Chinese
- Tradeoff: Inconsistent typography across devices/platforms
- Benefit: Zero font loading overhead, respects user's system preferences, optimized rendering

**Terse UI Labels vs Verbose:**
- Used concise Chinese translations
- Tradeoff: Less context in each label, might be unclear to non-native speakers
- Benefit: Natural for Chinese users, better UI density, follows platform conventions

**Same Character Limits Across Languages:**
- Chinese gets same 160-character bio limit as English
- Tradeoff: Chinese is more information-dense, so limit is effectively more generous
- Benefit: Simpler validation logic, consistent enforcement, allows expressive Chinese bios

**No CSS Typography Adjustments:**
- Didn't add Chinese-specific line-height or font rules
- Tradeoff: Typography might not be optimal for Chinese
- Benefit: Relies on browser defaults which are well-tested, simpler CSS, works well in practice

- Different character limits for Chinese vs English

- Pinyin romanization support or input method hints
- Regional Chinese variants (Cantonese, Shanghainese, etc.)
- Mainland China vs Taiwan terminology differences
- Chinese number formatting (e.g., 万/亿 for 10,000/100,000,000)

- Audio pronunciation for Chinese words

- Right-to-left text support (Chinese is LTR)

## 8) Known Issues / Follow-ups

**Traditional Chinese Support Not Included:**
- Current implementation only supports Simplified Chinese
- Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau users may prefer Traditional Chinese
- Could add zh-TW locale in future for Traditional variant

**TA Pronoun May Not Be Universal:**
- TA is progressive modern usage, not standardized
- Older or more conservative Chinese users might not recognize it
- Some may prefer 他 (generic) or 他/她 (dual form)
- Could add more pronoun options if user feedback indicates need

**Font Rendering Varies by Platform:**
- Chinese fonts look different on Windows (Microsoft YaHei), macOS (PingFang SC), Linux (WenQuanYi)
- No control over which font is used—depends on system
- Generally acceptable, but brand consistency affected

**No Chinese Font Optimization:**
- Not loading specific Chinese web fonts
- Could improve typography with Noto Sans SC or Source Han Sans
- Current implementation relies on system fonts which can be inconsistent

**Line Breaking Behavior:**
- Chinese can break anywhere between characters
- Proper line breaking requires language-aware algorithms
- Modern browsers handle this, but some edge cases might not be optimal

**Character Counter Semantics:**
- One Chinese character = one count, same as English character
- Chinese is more information-dense (1 character ≈ 1 word)
- 160-character limit in Chinese allows much more content than English
- Could consider different limits for different languages in future

**Measure Words Limited:**
- Only using 条 (tiáo) for comments and 人 (rén) for people
- Chinese has hundreds of measure words for different contexts
- More varied measure words could add linguistic richness

**No Pinyin Support:**
- Not providing pinyin romanization for Chinese learners
- Could add pinyin hints for non-native readers
- Not critical for target audience (native speakers)

**Mixed Script Rendering:**
- English names like "Mara Ortiz" appear in Chinese UI
- Sometimes causes awkward spacing with Chinese punctuation
- Acceptable in practice—common in Chinese digital products

**Search and Filtering:**
- No Chinese-specific search optimizations
- Pinyin search not supported (e.g., searching "beijing" to find "北京")
- Character-based search only

**Number Formatting:**
- Using Arabic numerals (0-9) instead of Chinese numerals
- Not using Chinese grouping (万 for 10,000, 亿 for 100,000,000)
- Standard practice in modern Chinese digital interfaces

**Input Method Compatibility:**
- Not tested with all Chinese input methods (Pinyin, Wubi, etc.)
- Should work fine, but edge cases possible
- IME composition events not specially handled

**Accessibility for Chinese:**
- Screen readers: Chinese support varies by platform
- No explicit lang="zh-CN" on specific elements (only on <html>)
- Could improve with more granular lang attributes

**Cultural Localization Beyond Language:**
- Date formats remain Western (YYYY-MM-DD) not Chinese style
- Time zones not localized
- Cultural references in seed data remain Western

**Performance with Chinese Characters:**
- Chinese characters take more bytes in UTF-8 (3 bytes each vs 1 for ASCII)
- Slightly larger network transfer for Chinese locale file
- Negligible impact in practice (~2KB additional)

**Copy-Paste with Chinese:**
- Copying Chinese text works fine
- Mixed Chinese/English might have spacing issues
- No special handling for this edge case

**Hong Kong and Taiwan Users:**
- Mainland Simplified Chinese may have different terminology than HK/Taiwan usage
- Some terms differ (e.g., "软件" mainland vs "軟體" Taiwan for "software")
- Could add zh-TW and zh-HK variants for regional support

**No Locale Detection for Chinese:**
- App doesn't detect if user's browser is set to Chinese
- User must manually select language
- Could implement navigator.language detection for Chinese users

**Emoji with Chinese Text:**
- Emoji mixed with Chinese text works
- Spacing might be inconsistent
- Generally acceptable—common usage pattern

**SEO for Chinese:**
- No Chinese meta tags or structured data
- Would be important for production Chinese market
- Not applicable for demo app

**Chinese Translation Quality:**
- Translations are functional but not professionally reviewed
- Some phrases might not be idiomatic for all contexts
- Native speaker review recommended before production use
